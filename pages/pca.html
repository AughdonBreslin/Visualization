Principal Component Analysis

Using SVD to understand principal directions, variance, and matrix decompositions

Overview

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a way to reorganize data around the directions where it varies most. If the centered data matrix is $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, PCA finds orthogonal directions that explain as much variance as possible, ordered from most important to least important.

In two dimensions, you can think of PCA as finding the best rotated coordinate system for the data cloud. One axis points along the dominant direction of variation, the other points along the remaining orthogonal variation. This makes PCA useful for visualization, dimensionality reduction, denoising, and understanding the geometry of a dataset.

PCA and SVD

For centered data, PCA is most cleanly computed through the singular value decomposition (SVD):

$$X = U\Sigma V^T$$

Here $V$ contains the principal directions in feature space, $\Sigma$ contains the singular values, and $U$ describes how the data coordinates line up with those directions. The covariance matrix is then

$$\frac{1}{n-1}X^T X = V\left(\frac{\Sigma^2}{n-1}\right)V^T$$

This is the link between SVD and PCA: the right singular vectors in $V$ are the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix, and the eigenvalues are the squared singular values divided by $n-1$.

Geometric interpretation

The PCA directions come from the covariance operator, which can be read as a sequence of geometric transformations. In two dimensions, the eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix is

$$C = V\Lambda V^T$$

This means: rotate into the eigenvector basis with $V^T$, stretch along those axes by the eigenvalues in $\Lambda$, and then rotate back with $V$. The walkthrough below shows both the data view and the operator view of that sequence.

Dimensionality reduction and reconstruction

After rotating into the principal basis, PCA orders coordinates by explained variance. Keeping only the first few components gives a lower-dimensional representation that preserves as much variance as possible among all orthogonal linear projections of that rank.

$$X_{\text{rank-}k} = U_k\Sigma_k V_k^T$$

This is why PCA is useful in practice: it compresses the data while keeping the dominant structure. When only one or two components explain most of the variance, the reconstructed data can remain visually and statistically close to the original while using fewer degrees of freedom.

Conclusion

PCA is ultimately a geometric and algebraic reorganization of the data. SVD gives a numerically stable way to compute it from the data matrix $X$, the principal directions come from the right singular vectors, and the explained variances come from the squared singular values. Conjugating the diagonal stretch by the eigenvector matrix explains why PCA can be understood as a rotation into a natural coordinate system, a variance-weighted scaling, and a rotation back.
