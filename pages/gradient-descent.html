The algorithms

Stochastic gradient descent

At each step, SGD moves the parameters directly opposite the gradient, scaled by the learning rate $\alpha$.

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \alpha \nabla L(\theta_t)$$

On an elongated bowl, equal step sizes in every direction means SGD bounces across the narrow axis while crawling along the long axis. The learning rate that is small enough to avoid divergence on the steep axis is far too small for efficient progress on the shallow axis.

Momentum

Momentum accumulates a velocity vector $v$ that carries the optimizer across flat regions and dampens oscillations across curved ones. $\beta$ (typically 0.9) controls how much of the previous velocity survives each step.

$$v_{t+1} = \beta v_t - \alpha \nabla L(\theta_t)$$
$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + v_{t+1}$$

On the elongated bowl, the accumulated velocity along the long axis lets momentum accelerate where SGD is slow. The overshoot into the steep walls eventually dampens, but the path oscillates more dramatically before settling.

Adam

Adam maintains a running estimate of both the first moment (mean gradient $m$) and the second moment (uncentered variance $v$) of the gradient. The parameter update is scaled by the per-dimension gradient variance, so large gradients get smaller effective steps and small gradients get larger ones.

$$m_t = \beta_1 m_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1)\nabla L$$
$$v_t = \beta_2 v_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2)(\nabla L)^2$$
$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \alpha \frac{\hat{m}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon}$$

The $\hat{m}_t$ and $\hat{v}_t$ are bias-corrected estimates that account for the zero-initialization of the moment vectors at $t=0$. Adam navigates elongated landscapes efficiently because the per-dimension scaling automatically compensates for the difference in curvature between axes. Its effective learning rate for each parameter is approximately $\alpha$, regardless of gradient magnitude.
